[center][b]A Space Launch System[/b][/center]

Here is a prototype of a launch pad to send balls into the space. The model is very idealistic, but perturbations of real-life factors can be compensated with a little additional cost.

Imagine a launch pad as a flat surface that kicks up [b]identical[/b] balls of diameter $d=0.25$ $m$ exactly vertically at the initial velocity of $v=30$ $m/s$. To be launched the ball is placed at the exact starting point on the launch pad. When a previously launched ball lands back on the launch pad it gets caught and is [b]immediately[/b] relaunched (at $v=30$ $m/s$). Let the first ball be launched at $t_0$. Then at each moment $t_n = t_0 + 1.2n$ $s$ where $n = 1,2,3,...$, the launch system adds and launches a new ball [b]provided that[/b] some previously launched ball will not be touched in the process.

For the sake of prototyping accept the gravitational acceleration always to be $g=10$ $m/s^2$. Neglect air friction and deviation from the vertical line. Neglect any deformation and energy loss at colliding balls: as soon as two balls collide they instantly exchange their velocities.

For the answer give the maximum number of balls to be flying before there will be no opportunity to add more (because at any moment some ball will be as close to the start point as to prevent it).
